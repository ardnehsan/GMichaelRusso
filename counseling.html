Statement of Counseling Theory

Integrative Neurotherapy

I believe that counseling promotes changes in the brain. Each time we learn something new, we create a new connection within our brain. Conversely, when we introduce toxins to our brain such as chronic stress, alcohol, or inadequate sleep, we damage these neurons and impede connections from being made. As such, my predominant theoretical orientation would be identified as integrative neurotherapy. This theoretical orientation builds upon the literature basis in multiple fields such as: counseling, neurology, physics, advancements in medicine, and many other disciplines. Specifically, my views regarding this work has been influenced by counselor educators such as: Lori Russell-Chapin, Thom Field, Laura Jones, Eric Beeson, Raissa Miller, Carlos Zalaquett, Allen Ivey, and Chad Luke. I had the honor to work with a number of these individuals and present on our collaborative work where we created and defined the term which encompasses my clinical practice: integrative neurotherapy. Integrative neurotherapy is the umbrella term which encompasses the approaches included in Neuroscience-informed counseling, neurofeedback/biofeedback, and adjunctive therapies or lifestyle changes which impact neural health (Field et al., 2019).

Neuroscience-Informed Counseling

Neuroscience-informed counseling comprises of a series of psychoeducational tools/techniques which empower the individual, or group, to increase their level of awareness about how their lifestyle can promote or diminish their interpersonal, emotional, and cognitive functioning. It is not uncommon for a counselor who is utilizing this approach to talk with the client(s) about the healthy mind platter, the diathesis stress model, the hand brain model, or the normal stress response. The healthy mind platter discusses ways that our brain changes as we engage in 7 areas: time in, downtime, physical time, connection time, sleep time, play time, and focus time (Rock et al., 2012). Although, as many of us as counselors are well aware, these topics are not new. Meyers et al. (2000) defined wellness “as a way of life oriented toward optimal health and well-being in which body, mind, and spirit are integrated by the individual to live more fully within the human and natural community”. As such, one function of neuroscience-informed counseling is to validate wellness through the discussion of neurophysiological underpinnings associated with a healthy life balance. From a practical standpoint, in my present work with college students, I often hear students present to counseling with the goal of decreasing the frequency and intensity of panic attacks. The first step in any approach would be to build and maintain rapport. Often times the second session is when we begin work. From a neuroscience-informed counseling perspective, we will collaboratively explore how they spend their time in terms of the healthy mind platter. This will provide a framework of their strengths and areas that we can collaboratively explore in sessions. I also have had the privilege to work with individuals with severe intellectual and/or developmental disabilities at a state-operated, residential facility. In this role, I was able to assist individuals who predominately communicated via their behaviors. As such, to empower a mindful approach, we played the game I Spy. This approach encouraged the individuals to enhance the period of time they spend focusing as well as being mindful of their environment. The exciting part about this was that the rate of violent behaviors (hitting, scratching, and slapping) decreased for individuals who spent 20 minutes of the day playing I Spy.

Biofeedback and Neurofeedback

Biofeedback and neurofeedback are areas which are not new to the counseling profession. Danskin and Walters (1973) were the first advocates within the counseling profession to discuss biofeedback as a tool which addresses clinical goals through physiological regulation. Building upon this literature, Sarnoff (1982) advocated for the use of EEG biofeedback to promote client relaxation. Meyers and Young (2012) also advocated for the use of neurofeedback, also known as eeg biofeedback, as a tool which counselors should consider using in their practice. Both of these modalities empower individuals to become aware of the messages that their bodies are telling them and to be able to create changes which address their clinical concerns. Coming from the lens of neurofeedback, we are able to conceptualize clients in terms of: 1) too much EEG activity in a particular region of the brain; 2) too little EEG activity or; 3) portions of the brain are experiencing difficulties in terms of communication (too much or too little communication). From this perspective, we might notice that excessive high-frequency beta wave activity in a particular region of the brain could be associated with anxiety (Dreis et al., 2015). We also might note that there is excessive alpha asymmetry in the frontal lobe of the brain which might facilitate negative ruminative behaviors associated with depression (Baehr et al., 1997). However, prior to jumping to a protocol, it is important to first complete a brain map, which is a form of a quantitative electroencephalogram (QEEG). The brain map allows us to compare electrical activity that our client’s brain makes to individuals in similar groups based on factors such as: age, handedness, and sex (Longo and Russo, 2016, pp. 99-101). Through this comparative process, we are able to identify what too much or too little really means. We then will pair these analyses with the symptoms the individual wishes to work on. It is important to note that nothing goes into the brain in a neurofeedback or biofeedback session; however, these sessions allow the individual to learn ways to create changes in their physiology to address their clinical goals (Russo and Novian, 2014).

Adjunctive Therapies

The third and final approach in integrative neurotherapy is adjunctive therapies. Adjunctive therapies could include the use of vitamins/supplements to promote an increase in nutritional absorption. Included in this category might be referrals for nutritionists, physicians, and various forms of exercise (gyms, yoga studios, and/or personal trainers). An important first step with this approach would be to refer the client to a physician to complete bloodwork which explores client hormonal and nutritional levels. This can be powerful in the conceptualization process and provide information about possible nutritional deficits which might impact the individual’s mood. Fontani et al. (2005), found that use of omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids aid in the reduction of anxiety, sadness, and anger whilst increasing vigor.

Clinical Experiences

The use of integrative neurotherapy has been informed by my practice in a variety of settings including: psychiatric in-patient hospitals, non-profit, private practice, school-based therapy, university counseling centers, as well as a state-run facility for individuals with intellectual and/or developmental disabilities. I have found that in each of these environments that the use of neuroscience-informed counseling and adjunctive therapies have been welcomed and encouraged. However, neurofeedback/biofeedback has its own limitations due to insurance reimbursement and costs. These experiences inform my current practice as well as serve an integral role in my teachings, research, leadership and advocacy. In nearly every avenue of counseling, I have found that there is a neurophysiological basis or approach which informs clients about the merits of wellness and assists them in identifying and creating changes which promote their growth.
