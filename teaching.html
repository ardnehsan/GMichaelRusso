Teaching

Philosophy

It is my belief that research is the voice of our profession. Research is conducted in a style which simultaneously serves the function of advancing the counseling profession whilst honoring those whom we serve. As such, it is my belief that research, especially that which is driven by clinical practice and is rooted in ethical principles, is one of the most crucial components of our profession. Furthermore, it is my belief that research should promote social justice. This inherently occurs as we decrease the disparity between clients who have resources to take part in counseling and those who do not. All too-often, many of us within the profession forget that counseling is a privilege. Often times, those who can gain the most from this additional support cannot afford the costs demanded of them. As such, we address a need in our community when we conduct research which provides those who cannot afford the luxury of counseling with free or a reduction to the cost of services. Furthermore, their voice, which is present in both quantitative and qualitative methodologies, establishes a platform of advocacy and assists in stamping-out stigma which adversely effects mental health. Principles of social justice and equity do not stop when we receive notification of acceptance for publication. It is my belief that publication is only part of this process. The next stage is to share this information with our colleagues through presentations. While presenting, we invite feedback and exert a challenge to our colleagues to carry the torch forward by providing services to additional populations. Additionally, we challenge our colleagues to evaluate the efficacy of our approach while providing services to individuals/groups which have a unique set of needs and worldviews. Ultimately, research informs our practice, establishes the foundation for our presentations/teachings, and informs policies which govern our collective livelihood.

Practice

This philosophical approach to research has been the crux of my academic practices to date. An example of this philosophy in action comes from the development of an article that I collaborated with my colleagues on in 2015. The origins of this article stemmed from the recognition that there was a high frequency of individuals who were seeking support in managing symptoms of anxiety at our university-based training clinic. This clinic provided masters-level counselors in-training with support as they honed their counseling and neurofeedback skills. Upon recognizing the need of our community, we sought and received IRB approval and established our research study. We wrote-up the findings of the pilot phase of our study and were able to successfully publish the article. For many of the authors, this was the end of the process. However, given my philosophy of client advocacy, I felt compelled to continue to share our findings with the hopes that our methodology would inspire clinical researchers to see if they were able to promote growth in their environments. Upon concluding one of these presentations, I was approached by a professor at a university which, at the time, was located in the part of the country which had the largest density individuals who experienced opioid dependency. Through conversation, we were able to identify a new population which had a significant need and had the backing of previous literature to support a potential study.

Research Agenda

Moving forward, I aim to build upon my previously conducted research, which largely encompasses the use of neuroscience as a tool utilized by counselors to promote client growth. The goal of this research is two-fold; firstly, to explore the use of neuroscience as a tool which further validates the practice of counseling and; secondly, to explore the efficacy of neurofeedback in the treatment of clients’ brain-based clinical goals. Acquisition of grants and alternative sources of funding are paramount to the development of this research track. This technology will also assist in the development of a neurofeedback training program as well as assist in addressing programmatic standards. Given the recognition that clinical research is oftentimes costly and time consuming, I will actively pursue additional approaches to advance our profession such as the use of neuroscience-related meta-analyses. These analyses will further inform future research practices by identifying populations which have not been addressed by previous research and address the evidence-basis for the use of neuroscience-informed practices.

Relevance to the Counseling Profession

Danskin and Walters (1973) were the first authors to suggest the use of biofeedback within the practice of counseling. Since then, The American Counseling Association (ACA), The Council for the Accreditation of Counseling and Related Educational Programs (CACREP), and the American Mental Health Counselors Association (AMHCA) all address neuroscience through their standards, interest networks, and/or designated space within their official publications. My research agenda seeks to build upon the rich history and institutional support present through ACA, CACREP, and AMHCA to evaluate the efficacy of these practices. Additional evidence of this comes from my intended dissertation topic which seeks to test a randomized, sham-controlled neurofeedback trial within a population of individuals who meet diagnostic criteria for generalized anxiety disorder. To date, a significant gap in the literature has been the utilization of a sham-controlled neurofeedback design which is based on principles of learning. If successful, this would be the first article to-date which utilizes such a protocol. In terms of policy implications, this research would address the basis which many insurance companies use to deny coverage to individuals who wish to access neurofeedback services offered by trained counselors. Furthermore, the methodology could establish a foundation to further explore the extent to which neurofeedback is beneficial in the reduction of other clinical symptoms.
